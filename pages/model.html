The model

This page is the theoretical reference for the tspaint generative model: local ancestry as a discrete character evolving up the branches of each marginal tree of an inferred tree sequence. It fixes the notation, the state space, the generator \(Q\), the root frequencies \(\pi\), the soft tip-emission noise model, and the two Markov structures (vertical and horizontal) that the method keeps separate. Inference (the blocked EM that fits these objects) is documented elsewhere; here we describe only what is being modelled.

The construction reuses the mugration / discrete-phylogeography idea — a discrete trait evolving under a continuous-time Markov chain (CTMC) on a fixed genealogy (Lemey et al. (2009)) — and computes likelihoods by Felsenstein pruning (Felsenstein (1981)). The novelty is not any single component but the synthesis: a generative ancestry-CTMC run per marginal tree of an inferred tree sequence, with an EM-learned generator and per-tip credibility, yielding a calibrated soft posterior over local ancestry.

Local ancestry as a character on the trees

An inferred tree sequence (from Relate, tsinfer, or SINGER) is a sequence of marginal trees, one for each non-recombining genomic interval, sharing nodes and edges across adjacent intervals. Every sampled haplotype is a tip in every marginal tree. We treat the population of origin — the ancestry — as a discrete character that sits at every node of every marginal tree and evolves up each branch under a CTMC.

The two things being modelled, kept deliberately separate, are:

Structure Axis Mechanism What it estimates
Vertical up a tree ancestry CTMC with generator \(Q\); Felsenstein pruning the ancestral-migration timescale
Horizontal along the genome ancestry tracts, carried by edge span (recombination structure) tract switching — no free rate parameter

This decomposition — recombination (horizontal, a topology change) versus ancestral migration (vertical, \(Q\)) — is the conceptual heart of the model and is discussed in Section 5.

State space and the generator \(Q\)

The ancestry state at a node is \(s \in \{A, B\}\) for v1, generalising to \(s \in \{1, \dots, K\}\) for \(K\) populations. Along a branch the state evolves as a CTMC with generator (rate matrix) \(Q\). For two states,

\[ Q = \begin{pmatrix} -q_{AB} & q_{AB} \\ q_{BA} & -q_{BA} \end{pmatrix}, \tag{1}\]

where \(q_{AB} \ge 0\) is the instantaneous \(A \to B\) rate and \(q_{BA} \ge 0\) the \(B \to A\) rate. For general \(K\), \(Q\) is a \(K \times K\) matrix whose off-diagonal entries are non-negative rates and whose rows sum to zero,

\[ \sum_{n} Q_{mn} = 0 \quad\text{for every } m, \qquad Q_{mn} \ge 0 \ (m \ne n), \tag{2}\]

so the diagonal is fixed by \(Q_{mm} = -\sum_{n \ne m} Q_{mn}\). The implementation enforces exactly these conditions (validate_generator checks squareness, Equation 2, and non-negativity; make_generator_2state builds Equation 1). The code is written to be generator-agnostic: moving from two states to \(K\) is a generator swap, not a rewrite.

Transition probabilities over a branch

The probability of ending in state \(s'\) after time \(t\) given start state \(s\) is the \((s, s')\) entry of the matrix exponential of \(Q t\),

\[ P(t) = \exp(Q\,t), \qquad P(t)_{ss'} = \Pr(s' \text{ at child} \mid s \text{ at parent}), \tag{3}\]

computed by transition_matrix. Because \(Q\) has rows summing to zero, every row of \(P(t)\) is a probability distribution for all \(t \ge 0\). Branch lengths \(t\) come from the node times of the tree sequence; root branches have length \(0\) by tskit’s definition and are handled through \(\pi\) rather than through Equation 3.

Root frequencies \(\pi\)

The state at the root of each marginal tree is drawn from the root frequencies \(\pi = (\pi_A, \pi_B)\) (more generally \(\pi = (\pi_1, \dots, \pi_K)\), with \(\pi_m \ge 0\) and \(\sum_m \pi_m = 1\)). \(\pi\) is a prior on the ancestry of the grand-MRCA — an essentially arbitrary ancestral lineage — so a uniform \(\pi\) is the principled default. \(\pi\) also appears inside the tip-emission noise model (Section 3) as the fallback distribution.

\(\pi\) is conceptually distinct from the generator’s stationary distribution \(\pi^{\star}\), the equilibrium solving \(\pi^{\star} Q = 0\) (stationary_distribution). The model does not require \(\pi = \pi^{\star}\); the root frequencies are a free prior, not the CTMC equilibrium.

Tip emission: a soft noise model with learned credibility

Reference haplotypes carry a sampled population label \(\ell_i \in \{A, B\}\); query haplotypes carry none. Rather than clamp a labelled tip to a one-hot indicator, tspaint gives each tip a Felsenstein emission likelihood vector that is a noise model parameterised by a per-tip credibility \(w_i \in [0, 1]\):

\[ \mathrm{emission}_i(s) = w_i \,\mathbf{1}[s = \ell_i] + (1 - w_i)\,\pi(s) \qquad \text{(labelled tip)}, \tag{4}\]

\[ \mathrm{emission}_i(s) = \pi(s) \qquad \text{(query tip; flat / root-frequency)}. \tag{5}\]

Equation Equation 4 is implemented by tip_emission (it forms \((1-w_i)\pi\) and adds \(w_i\) at the label index) and Equation 5 by query_emission. The credibility interpolates between two limits:

\(w_i\) Emission Equation 4 becomes Meaning
\(w_i = 1\) \(\mathbf{1}[s = \ell_i]\) (one-hot) hard clamp — the label is taken as truth
\(0 < w_i < 1\) a mix of label and \(\pi\) label believed, but the genealogy can override it
\(w_i \to 0\) \(\pi(s)\) tip effectively re-inferred from the rest of the tree (like a query)

So a reference whose own down-pass posterior dissents from its label over a genuinely foreign tract is the introgression signal — the same machinery, with no separate code path. Reference and query tips are the same kind of object; the only difference is the strength of the prior on the label.

Anchors, the Beta prior, and why the panel must not all float

The credibilities \(\{w_i\}\) are learned, but they cannot all be free at once. Letting the whole panel go soft simultaneously reintroduces unsupervised degeneracy — label switching, a \(Q\) / \(w_i\) trade-off, and collapse. Two guards are part of the model specification:

  • A trusted anchor set is held at \(w_i \equiv 1\) (the most-confidently unadmixed references), pinning the labelling so the rest can float against a fixed reference frame.
  • The remaining soft references carry a \(\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\) prior with mass near \(1\), so a label is believed unless the genealogy insists otherwise.

Under this prior the credibility update has the MAP form

\[ w_i = \frac{\alpha - 1 + a_i}{\alpha + \beta - 2 + a_i + d_i}, \tag{6}\]

where \(a_i\) and \(d_i\) are the (span-weighted) expected counts of the tip’s posterior agreeing with and disagreeing with its label, accumulated during inference. Equation Equation 6 is shown here only to make the role of the \(\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)\) prior concrete; how \(a_i\) and \(d_i\) are accumulated belongs to the inference description.

Why the state must be per-tip-per-tree

A haplotype is a tip in every marginal tree. If we kept one ancestry colour per individual and pooled the evidence across all trees, the latent state would have nowhere to vary along the genome and the estimate would converge to a global ancestry proportion — the wrong target. Local ancestry requires the latent state to be per-tip-per-tree, equivalently per-edge: free to differ at one locus from the next.

Concretely, the latent ancestry lives on tree-sequence edges. An edge \((\text{left}, \text{right}, \text{parent}, \text{child})\) carries a child lineage over the half-open interval \([\text{left}, \text{right})\), and a clade that persists across many marginal trees is a single set of edges with wide span (given a persistence-preserving front end). This per-edge representation is also the channel through which genome-scale autocorrelation enters: the same edge spanning many trees ties the ancestry state together across that span, without a separate smoothing parameter.

The two Markov structures

The model contains two Markov processes on orthogonal axes, both explicit but parameterised very differently.

Vertical: the ancestry CTMC

Up any single tree, ancestry evolves under the generator \(Q\) via the branch transitions \(P(t) = \exp(Q t)\) of Equation 3. Felsenstein pruning over a marginal tree turns these branch factors plus the tip emissions of Equation 4 into node-level likelihoods and posteriors. The vertical process estimates only the ancestral-migration timescale — how fast ancestry changes as one moves up into the past — and nothing about tract switching along the chromosome.

Horizontal: tracts carried by edge span, not by a rate \(\rho\)

Along the genome, contiguous stretches of one ancestry (tracts) are not modelled with a free switch-rate parameter \(\rho\) as in a copying/HMM model. Instead, tract structure is carried by edge span. A lineage’s ancestry can only change where its genealogical context changes — at the recombination breakpoints where edges begin and end. Between two breakpoints the local tree is identical, so there is nothing to switch against; the ancestry is constant by construction over the edge’s span. The recombination structure that Relate / tsinfer / SINGER already inferred therefore supplies the horizontal coupling, and \(\rho\) disappears as a free nuisance parameter.

Why this decomposition is more falsifiable than a generic \(\rho\)-HMM

A generic ancestry HMM lumps all state change into a single switch rate \(\rho\) estimated from the data. tspaint instead decomposes state change into two mechanisms with separate, identifiable channels:

  • recombination — horizontal, a topology change, handled entirely by edge spans (the inferred ARG);
  • ancestral migration — vertical, handled entirely by \(Q\).

Because the edge representation separates them cleanly, \(Q\) estimates only the ancestral-migration timescale and tract switching is handled only by the topology. This pins each mechanism to an observable structure rather than to one catch-all rate, which makes the model more specific — and therefore more falsifiable — than a generic \(\rho\)-HMM: the recombination breakpoints are not free to absorb migration, nor vice versa.

The deliverable: a calibrated soft posterior

For each tip (haplotype) in each marginal tree — equivalently each tip-edge span — the model’s output is the down-pass posterior over the tip’s ancestry,

\[ \gamma_i(s) = \Pr(s_i = s \mid \text{local tree}, \theta), \qquad \theta = (Q, \pi, \{w_i\}), \tag{7}\]

a soft probability, not a hard call. Because the local tree topology is shared across a span and the per-edge accounting ties states together along the genome, \(\gamma_i\) is spatially smoothed without an explicit smoother. Two emergent behaviours follow directly from Equation 7 and the emission model:

  • Graceful relaxation. When the local tree cannot discriminate — e.g. a query coalesces with both source clades at similar depth — \(\gamma_i\) relaxes toward the prior \(\pi\) (50/50 for a uniform two-state prior) rather than making a confident wrong call. This is a consequence of the generative model, not a bolted-on rule.
  • Missing information is distinct from uncertainty. A tip that is isolated over an interval (no parent, no children) is a root unto itself: the tree says nothing about its ancestry there. Its posterior falls back to \(\pi\), but the output marks this span as missing-info, distinct from a genuine 50/50 uncertain call — a real interpretive distinction for introgression mapping.

Modelling assumptions and their limits

The model is deliberately a mugration approximation: ancestry is treated as a trait on a fixed inferred genealogy. This ignores that genealogy and ancestry are jointly distributed (lineages in different populations cannot coalesce) — the structured-coalescent point that BASTA and MASCOT correct, and that SCAR (Guo et al. (2022)) addresses on inferred ARGs for a different deliverable (migration demography, not per-haplotype painting). For a v1 painting tool the naive version is expected to be adequate, but it biases \(Q\), and the inferred branch lengths feeding Equation 3 carry their own calibration assumptions; these limits are stated plainly here so downstream interpretation accounts for them.
